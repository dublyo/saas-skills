## Main Takeaways

Classic moat theory:

- A moat is a durable competitive advantage that protects attractive returns.
- Morningstar's common sources are switching costs, network effects, intangible assets, cost advantage, and efficient scale.
- 7 Powers frames advantage as benefit plus barrier. That distinction is useful for SaaS because many founders name a benefit but fail to prove the barrier.

SaaS profitability:

- Strong SaaS moats should show up in retention, expansion, pricing power, CAC efficiency, gross margin, or lower cost-to-serve.
- Net revenue retention above 100 percent suggests existing customers expand enough to offset contraction and churn.
- CAC payback and gross margin matter because a product can grow while destroying cash.
- Rule of 40 and Rule of X are useful lenses, but not substitutes for understanding retention, customer value, and unit economics.

AI-era defensibility:

- Generic AI features and model access are weak moats because competitors can use similar models.
- Data is not automatically a moat. It must be proprietary, fresh, permissioned, domain-specific, and tied to better outcomes.
- Workflow depth, system-of-record position, integrations, collaboration, trust, and operational data are more durable than standalone AI outputs.
- AI can strengthen a moat when it lowers cost-to-serve, improves outcomes through feedback, executes actions inside a workflow, and creates auditability customers trust.

SaaS product design:

- The moat must become product requirements: entities, permissions, workflows, integrations, events, billing rules, entitlements, analytics, tests.
- A moat that cannot be instrumented is probably only a story.
- A moat that does not protect profit may still be useful, but it is not enough to keep a SaaS business healthy.
